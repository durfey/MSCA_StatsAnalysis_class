Step 1: Simulate Normal Linear Model

## slope & intercept
a<-0.8
b<-0.1

## Model 1 (normal)
set.seed(458769)
X<-rnorm(n=1000,mean=3,sd=2.5)
Eps<-rnorm(n=1000,mean=0,sd=2.5)
Y<-a*X+b+Eps
LinearModel1<-cbind(X,Eps,Y)
plot(LinearModel1[,1],LinearModel1[,3])

Now we fit a linear model to the data:

Model1.lm<-lm(LinearModel1[,3]~LinearModel1[,1])
summary(Model1.lm)

The Q-Q Plot of residuals

qqnorm(Model1.lm$residuals,ylim=c(-8,8))
qqline(Model1.lm$residuals)

Interpretation of Q-Q Plot:

The points of the residuals seem to follow the line fairly closely. Towards the ends, it begins to falter, but not by a huge amount. Ideally, we want the points to follow the line, which represents the normal distribution, as much as possible. In this case, it is not perfect, but it appears to be fairly good.

Step 2: Simulate the same model, with uniform residuals

## Model 2 (Normal X, Uniform Eps)
X<-rnorm(n=1000,mean=3,sd=2.5)
Eps<-runif(n=1000,min=-4.33,max=4.33)
Y<-a*X+b+Eps
LinearModel2<-cbind(X,Eps,Y)
plot(LinearModel2[,1],LinearModel2[,3])

Now, the linear model fitted to the data:

Unif.lm<-lm(LinearModel2[,3]~LinearModel2[,1])
summary(Unif.lm)

Q-Q plot of residuals:

qqnorm(Unif.lm$residuals,ylim=c(-5,5))
qqline(Unif.lm$residuals)

Interpretation of Q-Q plot:

Having uniform residuals changes our Q-Q plot dramatically from what we observed in the plot with normal residuals. The points follow the line through the middle section, but then fall farther and farther away as we move towards either end. Here, we see a curved, S-shaped pattern. This indicates that the distribution of the data may have short tails at both ends.

Step 3: Different Transformations of the Output

Q-Q Plot of the output data (not the residuals, as we saw above).

## Q-Q plot of data output w/ uniform eps
qqnorm(LinearModel2[,3],ylim=c(-10,10))
qqline(LinearModel2[,3])

Square Root Transformation:

## Q-Q plot after square root transformation
qqnorm(sqrt(LinearModel2[,3]-min(LinearModel2[,3])),ylim=c(-10,10))
qqline(sqrt(LinearModel2[,3]-min(LinearModel2[,3])))

Log Transformation:

## Q-Q plot after log transformation
qqnorm(log(LinearModel2[,3]-min(LinearModel2[,3])+1),ylim=c(-10,10))
qqline(log(LinearModel2[,3]-min(LinearModel2[,3])+1))

Inverse Transformation:

## Q-Q plot after inverse transformation
qqnorm(1/(LinearModel2[,3]-min(LinearModel2[,3])+1),ylim=c(-10,10))
qqline(1/(LinearModel2[,3]-min(LinearModel2[,3])+1))

From the above Q-Q plots, we see that the ones that have transformed data follow near the normal line more cleanly than the original unmodified data.

Analysis:

From the linear model summaries, we see that the models utilizing transformed data have an advantage of having a more significant intercept parameter. The residual standard errors are also lower than in the original model. One area where the transformed models do not have an obvious advantage is the R-Squared values. These values are lower for the transformed models, but not by a lot. Also, the Q-Q plots associated with with the transformed models look better than the plot from the original model. Overall, using transformed data to fit a linear model seems to be advantageous.
