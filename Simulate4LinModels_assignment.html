Model 1: Normal X, Normal Eps

X<-rnorm(n=nSample,mean=3,sd=2.5)

LinearModel1<-cbind(X,Eps,Y)

Model 2: Normal X, Uniform Eps

Here, we observe the jumping around of the estimates of the standard deviation of residuals. This tells us that not only can the Eps wildly influence our data points, but that it will not even do so consistently. Since the amount in which the Eps will influence the data points cannot be determined or estimated, it makes the unpredictability even more so.

These results of the estimated rho-squared for the models shows us a few things.

First, calculating rho-squared with the theoretical method gives us a similar, but not exactly identical value as when using the lm function. This is likely simply due to rounding.

Second, the only model that gives us a decent determination coefficient (rho-squared) is Model 1. All the others are below 0.50, which is not very strong at all.

Lastly, the rho-squared for Model 3 is so incredibly low that it really shows how erratic the Cauchy distribution really is. It is much closer to zero than to anything we could consider a fairly strong value.

From the four models, we see that the assumptions that were used to generate the data greatly affected the model behavior and estimated parameters. Model 1, which utilized normal Eps, was by far the best and created the strongest linear model. Model 2, using a uniform Eps, resulted in a plot of points that look uniformly distributed between two parallel lines. Model 3 was easily the craziest of the bunch, resulting in an extremely weak linear model. The Eps generated with the Cauchy distribution were just too erratic and influenced the data too much and too unpredictably. Lastly, Model 4 appeared at first to look similar to Model 1 when it was plotted. However, calculation of rho-squared showed us that it is in fact much weaker than Model 1. This also tells us that just a little variation of Eps, even non-random variation, will affect the overall linear model.
